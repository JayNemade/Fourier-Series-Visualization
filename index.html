Fourier Series

Introduction

This project is on the demonstration of fourier series by visulizing the finite-infinite sum of sines and cosines. The concept behind the fourier series was given by a brilliant mathematician by the name of, Joseph Fourier. The idea of the original paper was that any arbitrary periodic function could be represented by a specific sum of sines and cosines.

The theme behind this webpage is that even a layman should understand the key concepts behind the fourier series so the language would be simplistic and using 'complex' mathematical concepts will be avoided. If you are already familiar with fourier series, you can skip to Instructions for use.

Cosine wave/function

On a Mathematical basis the cosine of a function is it's Adjacent side divided by it's hypotenuse.There is nothing much to it different than Sine wave. It's just phase shifted a 180 degrees.

Relationship between the osscilation and the sine wave produced by it

The Sine wave has some properties which we should be aware of. The sine wave has a wavelength λ and a Amplitude A.
Wavelength λ is defined as the time required for the function to complete one full osscilation. Thus λ is inversly proportional to the speed of the osscilation or the time period.
The Amplitude A is defined as the height of the wave. Therefore A is directly proportional to the radius.

Concept behind the Fourier series

A Fourier series is an expansion of a periodic function f(x) in terms of an infinite sum of sines and cosines. Fourier series make use of the orthogonality relationships of the sine and cosine functions. The computation and study of Fourier series is known as harmonic analysis.

Where, When and How to use this visualization

Where is the use of this project

The sole use of this project is in academics. Professors can use this visualization if they are teaching harmonic analysis. Professors can use this visualization to a more intuitive way to approach fourier series. It also gives a intresting touch to the so considered 'boring topics' like fourier series.

How to use the visualization

  • The sliders for the value of time period, radius, number of iterations are given just below the animation on the left.
  • As you change the values, the animation is morphed live and you can observe the effect of change in the different parameters on the fourier sum which is visualized in the animation.
  • The functions can be selected by the selection menu on the right of the sliders.
  • All of the sliders and selection menus are labeled for convenience.
